Connected Patient Analytics Platform

Your CPAP records dozens of signals per second each night. Other apps give you a usage score and a single AHI. Connected Patient Analytics Platform opens the raw signal — leak, pressure, flow, snore, events, every sample — and plots it as something you can actually read.

How it works

From SD card to readable chart in four steps.

The app expects a ZIP made from the root of a CPAP SD-card copy, keeping the full folder structure intact so it can find what it needs.

Remove the SD card

Power off your CPAP and safely eject the SD card.

Copy the contents

Drag the full SD contents into a folder on your computer.

Zip the folder

Create one .zip. Optional: trim old DATALOG/ days to shrink it.

Open in the app

Drop the ZIP in, pick a night, and the charts render locally.

A tour of one night

Same ZIP, ten different ways to read it.

Four tabs, in the order a curious sleeper usually hits them. Screenshots are from the built-in demo — not real patient data.

Tab 01 · Overview

The whole night, in one look.

Leak and pressure plotted together across the detected session — so you can see at a glance whether the mask held, where the pressure settled, and whether the two ever argued with each other.

Underneath, a summary writes itself for every night you pick: usage hours, 95th-percentile leak, median pressure, and how many events the machine flagged.

Why it mattersIf something looks off in the narrative, the tabs that follow are where you go to find out what.
Tab 03 · Pressure

Therapy pressure, straight from the EDF.

Other apps show you an average. CPAP plots every sample the machine recorded — the ramp-up, the holds, and the responses to breathing events as they happened.

Especially honest on APAP: you can see whether the machine actually adjusted all night, or quietly parked at one pressure and stayed there.

Tab 10 · Compare

Overlay signals from separate tabs.

Pick any combination of leak, pressure, flow, snore, respiratory rate, tidal volume, or exhalation pressure and stack them on one plot — normalized to the same axis, or kept raw — to see how they move together through the night.

Use caseLeak spike at 3 a.m.? Pull up leak and pressure on the same plot and see exactly what the machine did about it.
Tab 09 · Events

Every annotation your machine wrote down, decoded.

Obstructive apneas, hypopneas, RERAs, central pauses, flow limitations — whatever the machine chose to flag that night is parsed out of the EDF annotations, grouped by code, and charted with session-boundary markers.

A glossary underneath translates each tag, so you're not googling "CSR" at 2 a.m.

The other six tabs

Pulled from the same archive, no extra setup.

Whatever the machine recorded that night shows up as its own tab. You don't pick them — the app inventories the ZIP and lights up whatever's there.

Leak
Mask-seal behavior with the large-leak guide line drawn in — so you can see how long the seal was compromised, not just the average.
Flow
The raw breathing waveform. Flow-limited breaths and pauses jump out of the shape before the machine ever flags them.
Snore
The vibration-based snore channel the machine records but other apps don't graph.
Respiratory Rate
Breaths per minute through the night. Central pauses and arousals both leave a fingerprint here.
Tidal Volume
Air moved per breath, overlaid with male and female baseline reference lines so you know where "normal" is.
EPR
Therapy pressure vs. exhalation pressure with derived relief amount — the honest view of what EPR is actually doing on your curve.

Privacy & Scope

No upload. No account. No cloud.

This is a single local file. Your data is read into your browser and never sent anywhere.

What stays where

  • Runs locally in your browser. No data leaves your computer.
  • No backend, no account, no telemetry. There's nothing to sign up for.
  • Ships as one self-contained file so you can audit, archive, or open it offline.
  • Free and open source. The whole project is on GitHub for anyone to read or fork.
Not medical advice. Intended for exploration and curiosity — not diagnosis or treatment. If something looks off, talk to your doctor or sleep specialist.
How to get it

Ready to read your own nights?

The whole app ships as a single HTML file in the public repository on GitHub.

  1. 01
    Open the repository. Click the GitHub link below to visit the project on GitHub.
  2. 02
    Click the green <> Code button, then choose Download ZIP.
  3. 03
    Extract the ZIP. You only need CPAP.html to run the whole app — double-click it to open in your browser.

Tip: Drop CPAP.html directly on your CPAP's SD card. All you need is the SD card, a PC, and a web browser — the app and your data travel together.
